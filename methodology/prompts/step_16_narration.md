#### Global Diffusion of E-Procurement Innovations at the WBG (1998–2025)

##### Introduction
Electronic public procurement (“e-procurement”) has transitioned from a niche innovation in the late 1990s to a mainstream component of World Bank Group (WBG) operations by the mid-2020s. Table E.7 (the authoritative dataset for this analysis) shows a steep rise in WBG projects incorporating e-procurement over 1998–2025, alongside a declining share of those projects explicitly labeled as “innovative”[1][2]. In 1998–2007, 52 projects included e-procurement components (with ~21% of these framed as an innovation), expanding to 111 projects in 2008–2017 (~20% framed as innovation) and 121 projects in 2018–2025 (~15% framed as innovation)[1][2]. This report examines how e-procurement diffused globally through the WBG’s portfolio, interpreting the diffusion pathway using key concepts – the S-Curve of adoption, Champions, Isomorphic pressures (coercive, mimetic, normative), Reinvention, and the “Messy Middle” – as outlined in the provided “Diffusion of E-Procurement” framework. The analysis situates this diffusion in historical and institutional context, aligning WBG’s timeline with global shifts (from the Post-Washington Consensus and Millennium Development Goals to the digital transformation era and the rise of results-based management). It also scrutinizes how internal WBG processes (procurement policy reforms, organizational changes, staff incentives, and toolkits like MAPS and OC4IDS) facilitated or hindered the spread of e-procurement. Finally, the report explains why e-procurement’s framing as an “innovation” waned as adoption became widespread, and reflects on the institutional and epistemic significance of e-procurement’s mainstreaming within the WBG. Throughout, concrete country examples (Brazil, Philippines, Sierra Leone, Ukraine, among others) are used to illustrate the diffusion pathways.
#### The S-Curve of Adoption: From Early Adopters to Late Majority

The uptake of e-procurement in WBG projects exhibits an S-curve pattern of diffusion, characterized by an initial slow uptake, a mid-period acceleration (tipping point), and eventual plateau as the late majority adopts the innovation. In the early adopter phase (late 1990s to early 2000s), only a handful of pioneering projects experimented with electronic procurement. WBG project lessons from the late 1990s contain tentative references to e-procurement, and by the early 2000s explicit mentions began to multiply[3]. These early efforts – such as the Philippines’ Government Electronic Procurement System pilot and Brazil’s upgrades to its ComprasNet platform – were framed as experimental reforms aimed at reducing bid-rigging and improving transparency[3]. Such projects were typically isolated “pilots,” often enabled by visionary reformers or supportive task teams willing to try new technology in procurement. During this period, e-procurement was novel enough that when it appeared, it was frequently highlighted as an innovation in project documents. As Table E.7 indicates, about 21% of e-procurement projects in 1998–2007 explicitly framed the e-procurement component as an innovation[1], underscoring its perceived novelty at the time.
By the mid-2000s, a tipping point was reached where e-procurement shifted from pilot to mainstream practice. This inflection in the S-curve can be attributed to accumulating demonstration effects and crucial institutional changes. The number of WBG projects with e-procurement components more than doubled in the 2008–2017 decade compared to the prior decade (rising from 52 to 111)[1][4]. A convergence of factors created momentum: successful early adopters provided proof of concept, technology costs fell, and global pressure for more efficient and transparent procurement grew. Within the Bank, the 2004 revision of Procurement Guidelines was a pivotal enabler that signaled the tipping point – it formally gave electronic bidding equal standing with paper-based bids[5]. This policy change effectively removed an institutional barrier, legitimizing e-procurement in all Bank-funded projects and encouraging wider adoption. The mid-2000s also saw increased support for governance and anti-corruption initiatives under the post-Washington Consensus ethos, which cast e-procurement as a key tool to improve transparency and reduce fraud. As more projects in diverse countries (from middle-income to low-income) piloted e-procurement modules, a critical mass of knowledge and confidence developed, feeding a positive feedback loop: more staff became aware of e-procurement’s benefits (reduced bid times, cost savings, fewer complaints), and more client governments sought to modernize their systems.
Entering the late majority phase (late 2010s to mid-2020s), e-procurement became an established norm across WBG operations. By 2018–2025, 121 projects – spanning a wide range of countries and sectors – featured e-procurement elements[2]. At this stage, the innovation plateaued into routine practice: nearly all new investment projects with significant procurement components considered digital procurement platforms either as part of project design or as part of the country’s existing capacity. The S-curve’s upper asymptote was approached as even more reluctant adopters (including fragile states and latecomers) began implementing e-procurement, often with simplified models. Notably, even as absolute adoption rose, the share of projects touting e-procurement as an “innovation” fell to ~15%[2], indicating that the practice had become commonplace rather than cutting-edge. In other words, by the late 2010s, e-procurement was no longer seen as a special experiment but as standard good practice in procurement reform. This widespread uptake was cemented by the WBG’s 2016 Procurement Framework, which not only updated policies to promote value-for-money and sustainable procurement, but also embedded e-procurement into the Bank’s own systems. For example, the Bank introduced the Systematic Tracking of Exchanges in Procurement (STEP) platform in 2016 to handle procurement processes online, effectively mainstreaming e-procurement for all projects and linking with open contracting data standards[5]. By 2025, e-procurement had completed its journey from novel innovation to institutionalized practice, tracing the classic S-curve from early adopters to late majority within the WBG and its client countries.
#### Champions: Leadership and Advocacy in Diffusion

Throughout this diffusion trajectory, champions – both individuals and institutions – played a critical role in propelling e-procurement from idea to implementation. In the early stages, champions within pioneering countries and WBG task teams were essential. For instance, the Philippines’ early e-procurement pilot was driven by reform-minded officials in government in partnership with supportive WBG staff who championed the idea of an online government procurement service[3]. Similarly, Brazil’s advancement of its ComprasNet system in the early 2000s benefited from high-level political support and technical champions who framed it as a governance innovation to curb collusion and corruption[3]. These country-level champions demonstrated the feasibility of e-procurement, creating success stories that could inspire peers. Within the WBG, early adopter projects often had task team leaders or procurement specialists who acted as internal champions, advocating for trying out electronic tendering modules despite institutional inertia or skepticism. Such staff might leverage innovation funds or pilot programs and were often motivated by a commitment to improving governance through technology.
As diffusion accelerated, institutional champions within the WBG became more prominent. The mid-2000s saw the emergence of dedicated initiatives and knowledge-sharing networks for e-procurement, often championed by units like the Procurement Department or the Governance Global Practice. For example, after the 2004 guidelines change, the Bank’s procurement leadership encouraged staff to pilot e-bidding in projects, effectively championing the innovation at a policy level. Champions also arose in the form of cross-country knowledge networks: the WBG facilitated South-South learning where early success stories (e.g. Chile, Brazil, Korea – the latter outside WBG projects but influential globally) were showcased to other client countries. These networks, along with global forums and e-Government Procurement conferences, helped spread normative enthusiasm. Individual experts – often tech-savvy procurement specialists – traveled between countries, evangelizing e-procurement benefits and advising on system design. Their presence as respected professionals lent credibility (a normative push) and hands-on support to late adopters.
In the “messy middle” of diffusion (roughly mid-2000s to early 2010s), champions were crucial for sustaining momentum amid challenges. Some projects encountered setbacks (such as technical failures, user resistance, or slow uptake by suppliers), which could have stalled diffusion. Champions helped troubleshoot these issues, secure additional resources, and adapt approaches (exemplifying the reinvention concept, discussed later). For instance, when a country’s initial e-procurement attempt faltered due to low usage, WBG champions often advocated for policy changes (like mandating e-submission of bids) or additional training and incentives to increase buy-in. Moreover, champions within donor agencies and partner institutions – such as the Asian Development Bank, Inter-American Development Bank, and OECD – coordinated with WBG efforts, collectively reinforcing e-procurement as an international best practice. These inter-institutional champions created a chorus of support that countries found hard to ignore.
By the later stages, the role of champions evolved into one of consolidating and institutionalizing the innovation. Rather than needing maverick advocates to initiate e-procurement, champions in the 2010s focused on deepening and standardizing its use. For example, WBG’s Chief Procurement Officer and the specialists in charge of the 2016 Procurement Framework acted as champions by formally integrating e-procurement requirements into Bank operations (through STEP and new regulations)[6]. In client countries, early adopters like Brazil continued to champion new frontiers (e.g. running complex auctions via e-procurement platforms[7]) and shared their experience with later adopters through communities of practice. Even civil society and tech community champions (e.g. proponents of open contracting) emerged, advocating for advanced uses of e-procurement data to enhance transparency, which kept the momentum and political support high. In summary, across all phases of the S-curve, champions – from project-level enthusiasts to high-level policymakers – were the agents who pushed the diffusion process forward, navigated obstacles, and kept e-procurement on the agenda until it became self-sustaining.
#### Isomorphic Pressures: Coercive, Mimetic, and Normative Drivers

The global diffusion of e-procurement through WBG projects did not occur in isolation; it was propelled by isomorphic pressures – coercive, mimetic, and normative – that encouraged countries to adopt similar innovations in public procurement.
-Coercive pressures came from the policies and funding requirements imposed by powerful institutions. The WBG itself acted as a source of coercive pressure (albeit often gently applied) by embedding e-procurement expectations in its operations and policy dialogue. After 2004, the Bank’s procurement rules explicitly permitted and encouraged electronic bidding[5], and by the 2010s the Bank often included e-procurement components in lending operations as part of fiduciary strengthening. In some cases, loan conditions or technical assistance packages effectively required borrower governments to implement e-procurement systems (for example, a development policy loan might make disbursement contingent on launching a procurement portal, or an investment project might allocate funds specifically for e-GP rollout). Furthermore, global initiatives like the UNCITRAL Model Procurement Law (updated in 2011) and European Union directives mandating e-procurement in public tenders created external coercive benchmarks that influenced many WBG client countries (especially those aspiring to EU membership or following international standards). The cumulative effect was that countries felt institutional pressure – from donors, lenders, and trade agreements – to modernize procurement through digital means or risk being seen as non-compliant or left behind. Coercive pressure was also evident in WBG’s internal incentive structure: as the Bank began tracking procurement performance (e.g. through STEP), country teams were pressed to improve efficiency and transparency, implicitly coercing adoption of e-procurement as a means to those ends.
-Mimetic pressures significantly shaped behavior as well. As pioneering countries demonstrated success with e-procurement, their peers sought to emulate these perceived leaders. For instance, Brazil’s ComprasNet and Chile’s ChileCompra systems became well-known models in the 2000s; other Latin American countries, seeing the praise and results these systems garnered (such as increased competition and reduced prices), were motivated to imitate them. The Philippines’ early e-procurement pilot likewise spurred interest across Asia. In Eastern Europe and Central Asia, countries like Georgia and Ukraine observed neighbors (and especially EU countries) implementing e-GP and followed suit, partly to mimic what was seen as modern, efficient government practice. The WBG facilitated this mimetic diffusion by organizing study tours and knowledge exchanges: procurement officials from one country would visit another to see e-procurement in action, often returning convinced of the need to “keep up” with their peers. Such demonstration effects were powerful – governments feared falling behind in global rankings or missing out on reputational gains. By the 2010s, having an e-procurement system was a mark of a modern public administration, so even without direct pressure, many governments copied the trend set by early movers. Competitive mimicry also played a role: for example, when one country achieved faster bidding times and savings via e-procurement (as documented in project lessons), neighboring countries felt pressure to adopt similar tools to remain competitive in attracting business or reducing corruption[8].
-Normative pressures emerged from the professionalization and global consensus around e-procurement as a best practice. International organizations, professional networks, and donor forums developed standards and shared norms that elevated e-procurement from an optional experiment to a normative expectation for good governance. The OECD-DAC’s Methodology for Assessing Procurement Systems (MAPS) included e-procurement indicators, signaling that a “mature” procurement system ought to have electronic tools[6]. The WBG and partners published guides and hosted communities of practice promoting the idea that digital procurement increases transparency and efficiency – aligning with broader norms of transparency and open government. Notably, the rise of the Open Contracting Partnership in the mid-2010s (initially supported by the WBG) created a normative push for publishing procurement data online, effectively reinforcing the expectation that governments use e-procurement platforms to collect and disclose data. Procurement professionals worldwide began to share a common belief that embracing technology was part of their professional duty; associations of procurement officials (e.g. the International Public Procurement Conference, regional procurement networks) promulgated standards that made e-procurement a key benchmark. As a result, even in the absence of direct conditionality, many civil servants internalized the norm that an electronic government procurement system is a hallmark of a well-functioning, transparent procurement regime[9]. This normative shift was amplified within the WBG by internal training and knowledge events – staff were trained on the benefits of e-procurement and success stories, creating an expectation that project designs should include such components where feasible.
The synergy of these isomorphic pressures accelerated diffusion. Coercive pressures provided sticks and carrots (policies, funding incentives), mimetic pressures offered models to copy, and normative pressures created a shared mindset that legitimized e-procurement. Together, they reduced the perceived risk of adoption and increased the costs (political or reputational) of not adopting. Over time, these pressures made e-procurement less of a radical choice and more of an inevitable step for countries aligned with global best practices and WBG guidance.
#### Reinvention: Evolution and Adaptation of the Innovation

A striking feature of e-procurement’s diffusion is the degree of reinvention it underwent – the innovation did not remain static but evolved in form and function as it spread. Early conceptions of “e-procurement” in the late 1990s were modest, often referring simply to publishing procurement notices on a website or basic supplier registration databases. By contrast, contemporary e-procurement encompasses a suite of integrated digital modules and advanced functionalities. WBG project lessons reveal that as e-procurement diffused, it was continually adapted to new contexts and objectives, embodying Rogers’ notion of reinvention where adopters modify an innovation to better fit their needs.
In the early 2000s, the focus was on core systems – rolling out national e-GP (electronic Government Procurement) portals that could handle basic procurement steps online. Indeed, more than half of all e-procurement-related lessons in WBG projects concern the establishment of a central e-GP platform covering government agencies[10]. Typical reports from this era note achievements like “the project launched the national e-GP portal covering all ministries,” reflecting a primary concern with building the foundational infrastructure[10]. This first generation of e-procurement aimed to digitize procurement workflows (like advertisement, bid submission, and tender opening) to increase competition and transparency.
Soon after the core platforms were in place, specific modules and enhancements became the next targets of innovation. During the 2000s, many projects added online bidding or e-tendering modules, reducing paperwork and accelerating bid evaluation. Lessons frequently cited outcomes such as “electronic bidding reduced the average tender time from 90 to 45 days,” highlighting efficiency gains[8]. As countries gained experience, they started to bolt on new features: supplier registration portals emerged (some as early as the late 1990s) to broaden the pool of vendors by enabling easy online sign-up[11]. By the 2010s, reinvention took e-procurement into new territory aligned with evolving global priorities. There was a surge of transparency and open-data reforms attached to e-procurement – for example, integrating Open Contracting Data Standards (OCDS) so that data on bids, awards, and contracts are published for public oversight[12]. A project in Sierra Leone illustrates this: the rollout of e-procurement was explicitly linked to creating a public open-contracting portal disclosing contracts in real time[13]. This shows how the innovation was reinterpreted as not just a transactional tool, but a transparency and civic engagement mechanism.
Reinvention also occurred through integration with other government systems. Many countries adapted their e-procurement to link with financial management information systems (IFMIS) or payment platforms. By connecting e-GP with budgeting and treasury systems, governments could automatically enforce payment timeliness and audit trails. For instance, some WBG projects reported that linking e-procurement with the IFMIS “cut payment delays by half,” demonstrating the innovation’s adaptation to solve public financial management issues[14]. Likewise, contract-management dashboards became a popular add-on in the 2010s: e-procurement data feeds were used to monitor contract implementation, flagging delays or cost overruns in real time[15]. An example from Ukraine shows reinvention at an advanced level – Ukraine’s ProZorro e-procurement system developed an open API, which a WBG-supported road project then integrated into a GIS-based asset management dashboard, so that road repairs procured via e-procurement could be tracked spatially for progress[16]. This creative adaptation – feeding procurement data directly into a geospatial system for monitoring – was far beyond the scope of what early e-procurement pioneers imagined, epitomizing reinvention driven by local problem-solving.
Moreover, the innovation was tailored to different contexts and needs. In high-capacity settings, sophisticated features like reverse auctions and e-catalogues were introduced – for example, Brazil piloted reverse auctions via its ComprasNet platform and achieved significant savings[17]. In lower-capacity or fragile settings, however, e-procurement had to be reimagined in simpler, robust forms. A case from Liberia demonstrates this adaptive reinvention: facing limited internet connectivity and capacity, a project implemented a “light e-catalogue module” for common goods, hosted by a third-party (UNOPS) to ensure oversight despite low bandwidth[18]. By piggybacking on a donor-managed platform and focusing on a narrow functionality (catalogue purchasing), Liberia could still benefit from e-procurement principles even when a full system was not feasible. This kind of context-driven modification shows how the innovation’s content was not monolithic – countries reinvented e-procurement to fit their administrative realities, whether that meant a full-suite system or a streamlined workaround.
Reinvention was also influenced by paradigm shifts in technology and policy. The rise of cloud computing and mobile technology in the 2010s made it easier to deploy e-procurement solutions and broaden user access, so many countries updated earlier systems onto newer technical platforms. Likewise, evolving policy agendas – such as the push for open government, or for integrating sustainability criteria in procurement – led to new features like open data portals and eco-friendly procurement tracking in e-GP systems. The WBG and its partners supported these continual enhancements through toolkits (e.g. incorporating OC4IDS – Open Contracting for Infrastructure Data Standard – into e-procurement for tracking large infrastructure project contracts[6]) and pilot projects that tested cutting-edge ideas (such as using remote sensing to monitor procurement outcomes; one Afghanistan irrigation project used satellite imagery to verify work progress tied to e-procurement lots[19]). Each successful experiment expanded the definition of what e-procurement innovation entailed.
In summary, e-procurement as diffused by the WBG was not a static package, but a dynamic and evolving innovation. Through a process of reinvention, it adapted to new problems (transparency, integration, monitoring), incorporated new technologies, and fit diverse environments. This adaptability was crucial to its diffusion: countries and WBG teams could customize and extend the innovation’s value, which increased buy-in and ensured that e-procurement remained relevant over nearly three decades of reform. The concept of “e-procurement” thus continuously renewed itself – from basic digitization of bids to a platform for data analytics and beyond – which helped sustain the diffusion momentum.
#### The “Messy Middle”: Dynamics of Diffusion Beyond Endpoints

While it is tempting to view diffusion as a smooth trajectory from pilot to mainstream, the reality is that the path was characterized by a “messy middle” phase where progress was nonlinear, contested, and required iterative learning. This period roughly spanned the mid-2000s through the early 2010s – after the initial pilots but before near-universal acceptance – and it encapsulates the process dynamics that shaped how e-procurement spread, beyond just the starting and end points.
During this messy middle, adoption did not proceed uniformly across all countries or even within organizations. Some governments that launched early e-procurement pilots struggled to scale them up; others achieved quick wins in one area (e.g. e-bidding for simple goods) but faced pushback in others (e.g. construction contracts or decentralized agencies). There were instances of partial or stalled diffusion: for example, a country might implement an e-procurement platform but still allow parallel paper processes due to resistance from certain ministries or legal uncertainties about electronic signatures. Such hybrid situations required several years of policy adjustment and confidence-building to resolve. The WBG’s own approach during this period had to adjust as well – moving from isolated project pilots to a more comprehensive, system-wide support. The Bank learned that simply financing an e-GP system did not guarantee its use; complementary measures (training, regulatory changes, user buy-in campaigns) were needed. As a result, many WBG projects in this middle phase included substantial capacity-building components and change management efforts alongside the IT investments.
One hallmark of the messy middle was the interaction of various drivers and barriers, creating a complex landscape. Technical challenges often intersected with political economy issues. For instance, introducing e-procurement threatened entrenched interests benefiting from opaque procurement; thus reforms could stall due to political pushback or bureaucratic inertia. In some countries, powerful contractors or even officials resisted the new systems that would curtail opportunities for rent-seeking. Overcoming this required persistent effort, often with champions leveraging coercive pressure (like tying reforms to loan conditionality or government performance assessments) and normative appeals (framing e-procurement as part of an international anti-corruption standard). There were examples of rollback and relaunch: a few countries launched e-procurement, saw it falter due to low uptake or corruption scandals, and then had to relaunch with a new approach years later. Each such case added to the body of lessons that the WBG and others compiled – for example, emphasizing the importance of legal frameworks, or the need for phased implementation (start with pilot agencies, then expand).
Another dynamic in the messy middle was the learning and cross-pollination that occurred. Different countries tried different models (some built custom systems, others bought off-the-shelf software; some centralized all procurement, others allowed decentralized platforms). The WBG functioned as a knowledge broker during this time, capturing lessons from one context and applying them to another. This sometimes meant mid-course corrections: a project in one country might be redesigned mid-way to incorporate a best practice observed elsewhere (say, adopting a more user-friendly interface or adding an open data component) once evidence of success emerged. The diffusion was thus not a simple replication, but a collective learning process, rife with experiments, feedback loops, and course adjustments. The “messiness” also came from coordinating multiple stakeholders – IT departments, procurement agencies, finance ministries, vendors – each with their own interests and pace of adoption. Achieving alignment among these players often required iterative negotiations and the building of coalitions (for example, forming a multi-agency steering committee for e-procurement implementation to ensure everyone’s concerns were addressed).
Crucially, the messy middle was the phase where mid-term results and evidence started to surface, influencing the trajectory. As data from initial implementations rolled in, it sometimes challenged assumptions. Not all early e-procurement systems immediately delivered the expected cost savings or participation increases – some saw initial drops in bidder turnout as vendors adjusted to new processes, or only modest savings if the surrounding procurement rules were still flawed. This could have dampened enthusiasm, but the iterative learning process allowed adjustments (e.g. simplifying the vendor registration steps if they were too onerous, or blending e-procurement with procurement policy reforms such as updating standard bidding documents). Over time, enough evidence accumulated of positive outcomes – such as reduced bid evaluation times, fewer complaints, and anecdotal corruption reduction – that these mid-term results helped convince later adopters. But getting to that point required navigating through ambiguity and refining the innovation’s implementation in practice, which is the essence of the messy middle.
The concept of the messy middle also underscores that diffusion is not just about adoption, but about routinization. In this intermediate phase, even after a country “adopted” e-procurement on paper, the struggle was to make it the default habit for procurement officers and bidders. That meant focusing on user experience, providing ongoing support, and sometimes enforcing usage (e.g. making certain types of tenders only available through the electronic system). The WBG often found itself supporting governments in these softer aspects of reform during the messy middle: drafting change management plans, setting up helpdesks for the e-GP system, or phasing mandates (first make e-procurement optional, then gradually compulsory). It was a messy, iterative push towards making the innovation stick. Only when these efforts succeeded did e-procurement move into the final stage of broad normalization.
In summary, the diffusion of e-procurement was not a linear glide from zero to universal adoption. The journey involved a challenging middle period marked by trial-and-error, resistance, adaptation, and learning-by-doing. Recognizing this messy middle is important because it highlights the process dynamics – the coalition-building, the problem-solving, and the incremental victories – that were necessary to transform a promising innovation into a sustained norm across the WBG’s global portfolio. This also reminds us that the ultimate success (mainstreaming) was contingent on effectively managing and surviving this turbulent middle phase of diffusion.
#### Historical and Institutional Context of Diffusion

The timeline of e-procurement’s spread at the WBG (1998–2025) dovetails with several paradigmatic shifts in global development discourse and technology, as well as significant internal reforms within the WBG. Situating e-procurement’s diffusion in this broader context helps explain both the drivers of innovation and the pace at which it became mainstream.
##### Global Paradigm Shifts and Trends

- Post-Washington Consensus (late 1990s – early 2000s): In the aftermath of the 1990s, the WBG and development community embraced a “post-Washington Consensus” approach, placing greater emphasis on institutional quality, governance, and anti-corruption, rather than just market liberalization. This intellectual shift created a fertile environment for e-procurement as a governance innovation. James Wolfensohn’s famous “cancer of corruption” speech (1996) signaled that tackling corruption was a development priority. Procurement reform became a cornerstone of the good governance agenda, as public procurement was recognized as a hotspot for corruption and inefficiency. In this climate, electronic procurement was appealing: it promised transparency (all bids recorded and visible), a reduction in discretionary power of officials (via standardized digital processes), and easier auditing. Indeed, early e-procurement pilots were justified on governance grounds – they were “framed as governance experiments to reduce bid rigging and improve transparency”[20]. The broader development paradigm shift gave WBG staff and borrowing countries the conceptual backing to pursue such innovations, aligning with the new norms of anti-corruption and public sector modernization.
-Millennium Development Goals (2000–2015) and Development Results: The launch of the MDGs in 2000 put results and effectiveness at the center of development efforts. While the MDGs did not explicitly mention public procurement, their focus on efficient use of aid and achieving targets indirectly pressured countries and donors to improve public expenditure management. E-procurement entered as a tool to increase the efficiency and speed of procurement, thereby accelerating project implementation (important for meeting development targets on time) and saving costs (stretching scarce resources). The WBG during this period also championed a “Managing for Development Results” initiative, encouraging better monitoring and outcomes orientation in projects. E-procurement’s ability to generate data (on bidding times, prices, participation) meant it could feed into results frameworks and key performance indicators. For example, a project might include an indicator like “average time to award a contract” and use the e-procurement system to track improvements[21]. The MDG era imperative of getting tangible results thus bolstered the case for systematic procurement improvements and helped justify investments in e-procurement to skeptics: if digital tools could shave months off procurement cycles, projects could deliver schools, roads, or vaccines faster, contributing to MDG outcomes.
-Digital Transformation and E-Government Boom (2000s–2010s): The 2000s saw rapid advances in information and communication technologies globally, along with a wave of e-government initiatives. Many countries were creating national IT strategies, rolling out government websites, and digitizing services like tax collection and civil registries. Public procurement became a prime candidate for digitization as part of this broader digital transformation zeitgeist. The technology to conduct secure online transactions matured significantly (encryption, digital signatures, etc.), making e-procurement more feasible by the mid-2000s. The WBG and other donors funded numerous e-government projects, often with an e-procurement component, to harness ICT for governance. This meant that e-procurement was not an isolated idea but part of a global trend towards modernization of the state through technology. The presence of tech-focused funding and political attention (often Ministries of Finance or IT driving e-government) created synergy for procurement reformers to latch onto. Additionally, by the 2010s, the rise of the internet and mobile connectivity even in developing countries meant that a critical mass of suppliers and government staff could access online systems, addressing earlier infrastructure constraints. In short, the diffusion of e-procurement rode on the coattails of the global digital revolution – it was a logical component of broader public sector digitization efforts that the WBG encouraged worldwide.
-Open Government and Transparency Movements (2010s): In the early 2010s, the Open Government Partnership (OGP) and related transparency movements gained momentum internationally. Governments made commitments to make data public and engage citizens. Procurement data transparency became a hallmark of these efforts, epitomized by initiatives like the Open Contracting Data Standard (OCDS). E-procurement systems were the natural platforms to enable such transparency – they could publish tender and contract information in real time for citizen scrutiny. The WBG, as a founding member of OGP and a supporter of open contracting, aligned its programs accordingly. Projects in the 2010s often linked e-procurement with open data portals or civil society monitoring components[13]. This linkage further embedded e-procurement in the institutional fabric: it wasn’t just about efficiency, but about citizen accountability and open government. Global norms were shifting towards expecting governments to be transparent by default, and having a robust e-procurement system was increasingly seen as evidence of compliance with these modern transparency norms. Thus, the open government paradigm reinforced and re-energized the diffusion of e-procurement, framing it as essential for accountability and not merely an internal government upgrade.
-Sustainable Development Goals and Beyond (post-2015): The SDGs (2015–2030) continued the focus on effective institutions (Goal 16) and public service delivery. Additionally, they brought new focus on issues like inclusive institutions and sustainability. In this context, e-procurement’s mainstreaming helped address targets on transparency, accountability, and efficiency in public spending (key aspects of SDG 16). Furthermore, by the late 2010s, climate and sustainability considerations entered procurement (green procurement, etc.), and digital systems made it easier to incorporate and monitor such criteria in bidding processes. The WBG’s support to procurement reforms post-2015 increasingly emphasized holistic system strengthening – e-procurement was one element alongside professionalization, updated regulations, and initiatives like citizen engagement in procurement. Thus, e-procurement diffusion is part of the broader story of strengthening governance to achieve sustainable development outcomes.
##### WBG Internal Processes and Reforms

Parallel to these global shifts, the WBG underwent internal changes that either catalyzed or complicated the diffusion of e-procurement:
-Procurement Policy Reforms: The WBG’s own rules and policies set the tone for what innovations could flourish. The 2004 Procurement Guidelines revision was a watershed, as noted earlier – by officially allowing and encouraging electronic procurement methods, the Bank gave clear authorization for staff and clients to pursue e-procurement[5]. This removed a potential excuse for not adopting (before 2004, some might argue that Bank rules required paper bids). Later, the 2016 Procurement Framework was even more transformative. It replaced older procedures with a more principles-based approach and explicitly integrated technology: for instance, the framework’s emphasis on modernizing borrower systems meant the Bank could accept a borrower’s e-procurement system if it met certain standards, rather than using strictly Bank-run processes. The 2016 reform also introduced STEP, a mandatory online system for procurement plans and transactions in Bank projects, effectively forcing both Bank staff and clients onto a digital platform[6]. This had a snowball effect – as governments had to interface with STEP for Bank-funded contracts, it often nudged them to upgrade their own systems to be compatible or to use electronic means for other (non-Bank) procurement as well. In summary, these policy changes were institutional facilitators: they aligned the rules with the innovation, turned e-procurement from a voluntary experiment into a de facto requirement, and signaled top management endorsement of digital approaches.
-Organizational Changes – Global Practices: In July 2014, the WBG reorganized its operational staff into fourteen Global Practices (GPs) and several Cross-Cutting Solution Areas. Public procurement staff, who were previously scattered or part of regional teams, became centrally coordinated under the new Governance Global Practice (with a dedicated leadership for procurement, and later a standalone Procurement practice team within Governance). This reorganization had mixed effects on diffusion. On one hand, it facilitated knowledge sharing: procurement specialists worldwide could more easily share experiences of e-procurement implementation across regions, guided by global practice leaders who championed common tools. For example, successful approaches from South Asia could be quickly communicated to teams in Africa through internal practice networks. The GPs also launched communities of practice and thematic groups on topics like e-Government Procurement, thus institutionalizing a forum for lessons learned and technical standards. On the other hand, the upheaval of reorganization initially caused some disruption – staff rotations and new reporting lines could have momentarily slowed ongoing initiatives as priorities were reset. However, overall the creation of a global procurement community within the Bank strengthened the momentum for e-procurement by making it a shared agenda and not just a few isolated champions’ interest. It also helped integrate e-procurement with other agendas (financial management, information technology, sector governance) thanks to cross-practice collaboration.
-Budget Reforms and Funding Mechanisms: The WBG in the 2010s implemented internal budget reforms focusing on cost efficiency and increasing the use of trust funds for knowledge work. How did this affect e-procurement diffusion? In many cases, trust-funded programs provided crucial resources for e-procurement capacity building and tools. The excerpt above notes that trust-funded diagnostics and toolkits – such as MAPS assessments and the development of OC4IDS – were important in solidifying e-procurement’s mainstreaming[6]. Donor trust funds (from countries like Korea or the UK, which were keen on procurement reform) financed many technical assistance projects, e-procurement readiness assessments, and the creation of implementation guides. This influx of resources was a boon; it meant that WBG teams had dedicated funding to help countries design e-procurement strategies, conduct pilots, and train staff – activities that often fall outside the scope of a normal investment loan. Additionally, the Bank’s internal budget shifts put emphasis on results and innovation – managers began encouraging staff to leverage external funds and to show tangible outcomes. E-procurement projects, with their measurable metrics (time savings, etc.), fit well in this results-oriented budgeting, helping justify continued or expanded funding. However, there were also challenges: in some periods, tight budget environments meant fewer full-time staff could be devoted to long-term institutional reforms like e-procurement unless there was external funding, which could have caused uneven support across regions. Despite that, by aligning with donor priorities (anticorruption, digital governance), e-procurement initiatives secured the needed resources to propagate widely.
-Staff Incentives and Culture: The institutional culture of the WBG evolved over this period to become more receptive to innovation, including technological solutions. Especially in the 2000s, the Bank began award programs and recognition for innovative projects. Task teams that implemented successful e-procurement components often received attention and sometimes awards (for example, there were internal Innovation Awards, or recognition in portfolio reviews). This positive reinforcement incentivized more teams to consider such approaches. Additionally, procurement staff – historically seen as compliance-focused – increasingly gained a development-orientation, encouraged by leadership to be “solutions facilitators” rather than just risk gatekeepers. This culture shift made procurement professionals more eager to adopt tools that could improve development outcomes (faster procurement, better value), hence more open to e-procurement. Nonetheless, some internal inertia had to be overcome; early on, not all staff were comfortable with changing traditional processes. The rollout of STEP in 2016, which mandated all staff to use an online system, was in a sense a top-down enforcement that changed behavior through necessity. Over time, as staff became familiar with digital platforms and saw benefits (like easier data retrieval), the internal mindset shifted to where e-procurement was the new normal. The “share of projects framing e-procurement as innovation” declining is also indicative of this cultural normalization – staff stopped thinking of it as something novel to tout, and started treating it as an expected part of the project toolset.
In conclusion, the historical and institutional backdrop against which e-procurement diffused was highly influential. Global shifts in ideas (governance, results, digital era) provided motivation and justification for the innovation, while WBG’s internal reforms provided the mechanisms and support to implement it at scale. The alignment of these factors – external paradigm changes and internal readiness – was key to the acceleration seen in the mid-2000s and the consolidation by the mid-2010s. E-procurement’s journey at the WBG is thus a reflection of both changing times in development policy and deliberate institutional action to foster innovation.
#### From Innovation to Norm: Declining “Innovation” Framing as Adoption Rose

One of the paradoxical trends noted in Table E.7 is that as e-procurement was adopted more widely, it was less frequently described as an “innovation” in project reports[1][2]. This downward trend in the share of e-procurement projects framed as innovation (dropping from about 21% in the earliest cohort to under 15% in the latest) is not a sign of diminished importance, but rather a telling indicator of how mainstreamed and routine the practice became.
In the late 1990s and early 2000s, calling e-procurement an “innovation” was almost necessary to garner support. It was new, relatively untested in development projects, and needed strong justification. Project documents and evaluation lessons highlighted it as an innovative element to emphasize creative design and to draw attention to its potential transformative impact. For example, a project might have touted the introduction of an online bidding system as a novel approach to fight corruption or improve efficiency, explicitly labeling it innovative to signal that the team was pushing frontiers. This framing helped secure buy-in from management and clients by underlining the progressive nature of the intervention.
However, by the time we reach the 2010s, e-procurement no longer needed special branding as an innovation – it had proven its worth and was often part of the standard package of public financial management reforms. The decline in “innovation” framing largely reflects this normalization. WBG staff writing Implementation Completion Reports (ICRs) or evaluative notes stopped remarking on e-procurement as something exceptional, much as one wouldn’t label the use of computers or basic financial software in a project as an innovation by the 2010s. In practical terms, as adoption rose, e-procurement became a baseline expectation rather than a bonus feature. Projects in later years might include e-procurement simply as one component among many, without fanfare – perhaps mentioned in a list of activities but not singled out as particularly innovative.
There is also an epistemic shift underlying this trend. In the early phase, the concept of e-procurement was still forming – there was curiosity and debate about how to do it and whether it would work. By the later phase, a solid evidence base and collective experience existed, so it moved from the realm of exploratory innovation to the realm of established knowledge. The discourse within the Bank and among practitioners had evolved: by 2018, one would discuss how best to implement e-procurement (which platform, what sequence, how to integrate data standards) rather than whether to implement it at all. The decreasing use of the word “innovation” parallels the journey of many technologies: once an innovation diffuses broadly, it ceases to be viewed as innovative – not because it failed, but because it succeeded to the point of ubiquity. In organizational terms, what was once novel becomes “business as usual.”
It’s important to note that this decline in innovation framing does not imply that e-procurement was less valued – on the contrary, it was valued enough to be universally adopted and no longer required special promotion. In fact, one could argue the decline is a marker of maturity: WBG projects only emphasize an innovation label when something is outside the norm; thus, not mentioning it indicates that e-procurement had become the norm. Additionally, the WBG and development community likely shifted their innovation spotlight to newer frontiers (for example, in the late 2010s, terms like big data, artificial intelligence in governance, or fintech might have taken the mantle of “innovation”), while e-procurement moved into the category of foundational infrastructure.
Finally, this trend speaks to the institutionalization of learning. Early on, calling e-procurement an innovation also signified that it was experimental and that lessons were being learned. By the end, the lessons had been learned and codified: the practice was embedded in guidelines, toolkits, and standard training. The system had absorbed the innovation. Therefore, project teams might have focused their write-ups on more problematic or truly innovative aspects of projects, rather than on e-procurement which was by then a known quantity. In summary, the decline in framing e-procurement as an innovation is a natural byproduct of its success – a shift from innovative to normative status – illustrating how yesterday’s innovation can become today’s routine good practice as diffusion reaches its culmination.
#### Diffusion Pathways in Practice: Country Examples

The global diffusion of e-procurement through WBG operations can be illustrated by looking at concrete country cases that represent different stages and pathways of adoption. Brazil, the Philippines, Ukraine, and Sierra Leone (among others) offer instructive examples of how the innovation spread, was adapted, and became entrenched under varying conditions.
-Philippines: Early Adoption and Demonstration Effect – The Philippines was among the early movers in e-procurement within the WBG context. In the early 2000s, with support from both the WBG and the Asian Development Bank, the Philippines launched its Government Electronic Procurement System (PhilGEPS). This initiative is noted in WBG lesson reviews as one of the “early pilots” in e-procurement[3]. It was driven by a strong domestic reform coalition in response to public outcry over procurement corruption, and it aligned with a broader government modernization program. The WBG-supported procurement reform project in the Philippines treated e-procurement as a flagship innovation to increase transparency – for example, by posting all bid opportunities and awards online for public viewing. The success of PhilGEPS (which eventually became mandatory for government agencies) had a demonstration effect in the region. Bank teams from other countries in East Asia (and beyond) often cited the Philippines example to justify similar components in their projects. The Philippines also championed the cause in international forums, sharing knowledge on how e-procurement helped cut red tape and reduce opportunities for graft. The pathway here highlights the importance of a conducive political climate and donor coordination early on – with the Philippines’ case showing that when a country’s leadership and external partners align on an innovation, it can create a model others seek to follow.
-Brazil: Indigenous Innovation and Continuous Evolution – Brazil presents a slightly different narrative. Brazil’s federal e-procurement system, ComprasNet, was largely an indigenous innovation, initiated in the late 1990s and expanded in the early 2000s, partly in response to fiscal pressures and a national transparency law. While not originally a WBG-funded project, the Bank recognized Brazil’s success and started to incorporate support for subnational e-procurement in its lending. For instance, a WBG Development Policy Loan (DPL) with the state of Acre noted the achievement that “the state PPP unit completed its first concession e-auction on the ComprasNet platform”[7]. This example from a Brazil project shows how a mature e-procurement system was leveraged for advanced uses like public-private partnership (PPP) auctions. Brazil’s pathway underscores continuous innovation – even after establishing the basics, the country kept expanding functionality (introducing reverse auctions, online catalogs, integration with financial systems, etc.). The WBG used Brazil as an exemplar in its knowledge materials, and Brazilian experts contributed to regional training, thereby diffusing the model. The role of champions is evident too: key Brazilian officials advocated the system’s benefits internationally. Brazil’s experience also introduced the idea of re-injection of innovation from clients back into WBG practices – learning from a client’s home-grown system (ComprasNet) influenced WBG’s own thinking and advice to other countries. Over time, features pioneered by Brazil (like reverse auctions) were piloted in other WBG client countries (e.g., some Eastern European countries tried similar auction methods in procurement following Brazil’s success). Thus, Brazil’s case exemplifies a diffusion pathway where a large emerging economy’s innovation radiated outward, with the WBG acting as a conduit for transferring those lessons abroad.
-Ukraine: Leapfrogging through Crisis and Open Contracting – Ukraine’s adoption of e-procurement in the mid-2010s is a dramatic example of diffusion accelerated by crisis and civil society engagement, later supported and institutionalized by WBG projects. After the 2014 Revolution of Dignity, Ukraine faced public demand for transparency and an urgent need to rebuild trust in government. A coalition of reformers and tech activists created ProZorro, an open-source e-procurement system, initially as a volunteer-driven effort. The WBG, recognizing the potential, provided technical and financial support to scale up ProZorro and integrate it into government formally. In a short span, Ukraine leapfrogged from rudimentary procurement to a world-renowned digital system that won international awards. A notable aspect of Ukraine’s case is the integration of open data and analytics from the get-go. WBG’s engagement (through lending and advisory services) helped Ukraine link ProZorro with broader public investment management. For example, as noted in a WBG project performance assessment, ProZorro’s API was used to feed data into a GIS-based road management system[16] – meaning Ukraine didn’t just adopt e-procurement, but used it as a platform for innovative integrations (tracking procurement outcomes in real time on maps, engaging citizens in monitoring construction progress, etc.). The Ukraine pathway illustrates how diffusion can be propelled by internal demand for change and then amplified by external support. It also shows the power of normative pressure: Ukraine was partially driven by a desire to meet EU standards and to break with a past corruption image, embracing open contracting principles fervently. The success in Ukraine became a case study that the WBG and Open Contracting Partnership showcased globally, influencing countries as far afield as Colombia and Vietnam to consider similar open-by-design approaches. In short, Ukraine demonstrates a latecomer advantage – adopting the latest version of the innovation (with open data and API features) and arguably surpassing earlier adopters in certain aspects, which in turn reshaped the expectations for other countries.
-Sierra Leone: Incremental Approach in a Fragile Context – Sierra Leone provides an example of diffusion in a low-income, fragile setting, highlighting an incremental and partnership-heavy pathway. The country’s post-conflict recovery in the 2000s focused on basic governance restoration, and only by the 2010s did e-procurement enter the agenda. Through a WBG Public Financial Management Improvement project, Sierra Leone initiated a modest e-procurement rollout. Given capacity and infrastructure constraints, the approach was phased – starting with establishing an electronic procurement portal for publishing tenders, then moving to pilot e-submission in select agencies. A significant achievement, captured in project lessons, was linking the new e-procurement system to an open data portal that disclosed contract awards in real time[13]. This marriage of e-procurement with transparency tools was done in partnership with donors and NGOs, illustrating that in fragile states the diffusion often relies on coalitions of support (the project leveraged a multi-donor trust fund and technical expertise from organizations specializing in open data). Sierra Leone’s example shows the importance of adaptation – the system was deliberately kept “light” and context-appropriate, and the reformers worked within political-economy limits, gradually winning stakeholder trust. By demonstrating even small improvements (like faster information access for suppliers and civil society oversight on a few pilot contracts), Sierra Leone built momentum for expansion. The WBG used this success to encourage other fragile states, suggesting that e-procurement was not only for advanced middle-income countries but could be tailored to work in low-resource environments too. The pathway here is characterized by patience, heavy capacity building, and aligning e-procurement with broader public finance reforms (so it was part of a comprehensive improvement of transparency and accountability post-Ebola crisis). Sierra Leone underscores that diffusion can happen even in challenging environments if the innovation is broken into manageable steps and linked to immediate governance gains.
These examples, alongside others (such as Afghanistan’s use of remote sensing with e-procurement, Liberia’s minimalist e-catalog approach, Georgia’s swift nationwide e-GP adoption, etc.), collectively paint a picture of multiple pathways converging towards the same end – widespread e-procurement. Some pathways were top-down (driven by government mandates and donor requirements, as seen in Georgia or Rwanda), others were bottom-up (driven by citizen demand and tech communities, as in Ukraine). Some were gradual (Sierra Leone), others big-bang (Korea’s full-scale launch, which served as inspiration though outside the WBG’s direct portfolio). The WBG’s role flexed accordingly: financier, knowledge broker, convenor, and standard-setter. By weaving together these narratives, we see that while the diffusion followed the general S-curve shape globally, there was rich variation in how different contexts navigated the journey. Each country example adds depth to our understanding of diffusion – demonstrating the roles of champions, the effect of pressures, the necessity of reinvention, and the reality of the messy middle in real-world settings.
#### Significance of Mainstreaming: Institutional and Epistemic Impacts

The mainstreaming of e-procurement within the WBG and its client countries by 2025 carries significant institutional and epistemic implications. It represents more than just the successful scale-up of a particular tool; it signifies a shift in how the WBG and the development community conduct business, make decisions, and conceive of innovation.
Institutional Significance for the WBG: From an institutional perspective, e-procurement’s integration into standard practice reflects the WBG’s evolution into a more modern, data-driven, and transparency-oriented development institution. One immediate impact is on operational efficiency and oversight: with e-procurement (and systems like STEP) in place, the Bank can monitor procurement processes in real time across its portfolio, flagging delays or issues earlier. This has improved project supervision and risk management, as procurement bottlenecks (a perennial cause of implementation delays) are now more visible and can be addressed proactively. Mainstreaming has also led to cost savings and consistency – for instance, standardizing on electronic processes reduces the transaction costs associated with paper bidding and makes it easier to apply uniform procurement standards across countries.
Moreover, the WBG’s institutional norms and procedures have been reshaped. Procurement is no longer seen as a standalone compliance function, but as an integrated part of development solutions. The fact that e-procurement is mainstream means that procurement reform is often automatically considered in broader governance or sector reform projects (health, transport, etc.), which wasn’t always the case before. The Bank’s internal training for staff now routinely includes digital procurement modules, ensuring new generations of staff have the mindset and skills to work with these systems. In addition, by mainstreaming e-procurement, the WBG has solidified its role as a leader in transparency and anti-corruption efforts. It can credibly advocate good governance when it has helped implement these systems in so many countries; this enhances the Bank’s legitimacy and soft power in policy dialogues. Also, mainstreaming implies that WBG procurement processes themselves (for example, procurement under Bank-executed trust funds or corporate procurement) increasingly mirror the advice given to clients – aligning practice with preaching, which strengthens institutional credibility.
Another institutional effect is on collaboration and data openness. With e-procurement systems generating vast amounts of data, the WBG has been able to support initiatives like analyzing procurement data for value-for-money, benchmarking countries’ performance, and opening data to the public (with client consent). This fosters a culture of evidence-based decision-making internally. It also pushes the institution into new partnerships – for example, collaborating with organizations like Transparency International or Open Contracting Partnership to utilize the data and improve outcomes, something that wouldn’t be possible without mainstreamed e-procurement systems. In essence, e-procurement’s mainstreaming is a cornerstone in the WBG’s broader shift towards being a “Solutions Bank” that leverages technology and data to enhance development impact, rather than relying solely on traditional finance and advice.
Epistemic Significance: Epistemically, the journey of e-procurement from novel innovation to mainstream practice reflects how the WBG and development field update their collective knowledge and norms. It shows the process of knowledge institutionalization: what begins as tacit knowledge in a few pilot projects (e.g., how to run an e-bidding) becomes documented best practice and eventually part of the default toolkit for practitioners. This progression has implications for how innovation is viewed. The fact that e-procurement is no longer called “innovative” by 2025 (as discussed earlier) indicates that the epistemic community of development professionals now treats it as an established truth – a normal part of how public procurement should be done. Such a shift can be self-reinforcing; when an idea becomes mainstream, academic curricula, training programs, and professional standards adjust to include it. Indeed, by the 2020s, one finds public procurement certification courses and government school curricula teaching e-procurement as a core module, not an optional curiosity. The WBG contributed to this epistemic shift by publishing toolkits, evaluation reports, and guidance notes capturing the lessons of two decades, thus codifying the knowledge around e-procurement.
Another epistemic outcome is a refinement in understanding what innovation in development entails. The mainstreaming of e-procurement exemplifies that innovations in the development sector often have a shelf life as innovations – they eventually mature. This has led to a more nuanced view where innovation is seen as a continuous process rather than a one-off introduction of a new gadget. As e-procurement became common knowledge, the discourse shifted to second-order questions: How to innovate within e-procurement (e.g., using AI for bid evaluation, or blockchain for procurement ledgers)? What new innovations can leverage the now-ubiquitous digital procurement infrastructure? In this way, e-procurement’s mainstreaming laid the foundation for further innovation. For example, with many countries having e-procurement, researchers and practitioners started exploring big data analytics on procurement datasets (to identify collusion or measure savings) – a frontier that would not be accessible without e-proc systems in place.
Epistemically, the mainstreaming also reinforced the importance of context in innovation diffusion theory. The WBG’s experience showed that while the idea (e-procurement) was global, its implementation needed context-specific knowledge – something acknowledged and absorbed by the institution. This led to a richer internal knowledge base: the Bank accumulated expertise not just on e-procurement technology, but on change management, legal adaptations, and sequencing of reforms in different environments (federal states vs. centralized ones, fragile vs. stable, etc.). Such knowledge is now part of the collective wisdom that the WBG brings to new challenges. In essence, the organization learned how to learn and adapt innovations, which is an epistemic skill transferrable to other emerging innovations.
Finally, the mainstreaming of e-procurement has symbolic significance. It demonstrates that large bureaucratic systems (both in governments and in the WBG itself) are capable of absorbing digital innovations at scale, albeit over a protracted period. This success story contributes to a narrative within the development epistemic community that complex institutional innovations are possible and can become the norm – a narrative that can inspire confidence when approaching the next big reform. It also raises interesting reflections on the definition of innovation: by 2025, some practitioners might joke that e-procurement is “old hat.” But this very sentiment is a marker of having collectively moved the goalposts of what constitutes innovation in public procurement.
In summary, the mainstreaming of e-procurement by 2025 has solidified improvements in how the WBG and its clients operate (institutional impact) and has advanced the collective understanding and expectations regarding technology in governance (epistemic impact). E-procurement’s diffusion journey has thus not only changed procurement processes on the ground but also transformed mindsets – making the once-unorthodox idea of digital procurement an unquestioned part of the development lexicon and paving the way for future innovations to be embraced in a similar fashion.
#### Conclusion

Between 1998 and 2025, e-procurement evolved from a fringe experiment to a foundational element of the World Bank Group’s approach to improving public sector governance. Guided by an S-curve adoption trajectory, the innovation passed through early adopter trials, a challenging growth phase, and eventual maturation into late-majority acceptance. Key concepts from diffusion theory illuminate this journey: champions were indispensable in advocating and institutionalizing e-procurement at every stage; isomorphic pressures (from Bank policies, peer examples, and global norms) created an environment where countries were encouraged – even compelled – to converge on electronic procurement as a standard; reinvention of the innovation ensured its relevance and adaptability, as e-procurement’s scope expanded from simple digital tenders to integrated, data-rich procurement ecosystems; and the messy middle reminds us that the path was not linear, requiring perseverance, learning, and adaptation to overcome resistance and complexities.
Historically, the diffusion of e-procurement cannot be separated from the broader shifts in development thinking and the digital revolution. It aligned perfectly with the post-Washington Consensus emphasis on governance and anti-corruption, gained urgency from the results-focus of the MDG era, and took flight on the wings of the global digital transformation and open government movements. Institutionally, the WBG’s own evolution – through policy reforms in 2004 and 2016, organizational restructuring, and an increasing openness to technological solutions – provided the scaffolding to take e-procurement from pilot to paradigm. The fact that by the late 2010s WBG staff and borrowers saw e-procurement as “the new normal” (and correspondingly stopped referring to it as an innovation) is testament to its successful mainstreaming[1][2].
Country experiences like the Philippines, Brazil, Ukraine, and Sierra Leone illustrate the diverse pathways through which this diffusion occurred – from early demonstration effects to late-stage leapfrogging – and highlight that while the destination was common, each journey was unique. These cases also reflect the tangible development gains from e-procurement: enhanced transparency (e.g. real-time public disclosure of contracts in Sierra Leone[13]), improved efficiency (e.g. tender processing times halved in various countries[8]), fiscal savings (e.g. via competitive e-auctions in Brazil[17]), and better accountability (integration of procurement with monitoring systems as in Ukraine[16]). Such results have reinforced the value proposition of e-procurement, further embedding it into the fabric of development practice.
By 2025, the institutional and epistemic significance of e-procurement’s mainstreaming is profound. The WBG and its clients now possess not just new software systems, but a transformed outlook on how transparency and technology can improve governance. The success of this diffusion has freed up the term “innovation” to be applied to the next generation of ideas, even as e-procurement continues to quietly deliver benefits in the background. In a sense, the story of e-procurement at the WBG is a microcosm of how a development innovation, through strategic support and adaptation, can go global – ultimately changing the “rules of the game” by becoming part of them. The lessons from this journey will inform how future innovations – whatever the next e-procurement might be – are nurtured from infancy to institutional adulthood in the pursuit of effective, accountable development.
